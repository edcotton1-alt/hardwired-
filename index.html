I
The
Finding.
Thesis

What sixty public companies revealed about preparedness for the next generation of AI — and why the strongest were not the loudest adopters, but the ones who had turned difference into structure.

01 The question

This began as a simple question: why do some companies look better prepared for the next generation of AI than others?

Not better prepared in the cosmetic sense. Not more press releases or pilots or "AI-powered" language in the annual report. Prepared in the deeper sense: able to use the technology without becoming generic, able to defend what makes them valuable, and able to grow from a difference that still matters.

So I looked at 60 public companies. The test was structural. Did the company understand its valuable difference? Was that difference still load-bearing under advanced LLM conditions? Had it built infrastructure to protect and evolve it?

The results did not show that AI companies win and old companies lose. They showed something more interesting. The companies best prepared for cheap intelligence were often those that had already turned difference into structure.

Chapter Two of Three / 02 Hardwired № 001 · April 2026
II
The
Diagnosis.
Thesis

Why the CMO has long been asked to manufacture distinctiveness without owning the systems that create it — and why cheap intelligence turns that old problem into an urgent one.

02 The impossible brief

Make us famous. Make us trusted. Make us premium.

CMOs have long been asked to perform a small miracle — make us famous, trusted, premium, distinctive. Shift perception. Create demand. Drive growth.

None of this goes away. Brand still has to be known to be chosen. But there has always been a catch. In many companies, the CMO is made responsible for distinctiveness while the real sources of distinctiveness sit somewhere else: product, service, pricing, supply chain, retail experience, data, operations, culture.

Brand became the wrapper. The serious work lived in other rooms. The one thing that joined all the silos, and made the company valuable, became nobody's full responsibility: difference. That was always a problem. Cheap intelligence is making it dangerous.

03 The hidden moats are weakening

For much of corporate history, brands were protected by frictions they barely noticed. Production was expensive. Expertise was scarce. Comparison was slow. Distribution was controlled. Customer memory mattered. LLMs weaken all of those protections.

Fig. 02 / The Weakening Moats
Old protections, new conditions
Frictions that used to protect
Production cost — Copy, code, content and analysis were expensive to make well.
Expertise scarcity — Domain knowledge was slow and costly to replicate.
Comparison friction — Evaluating alternatives took time; inertia favoured incumbents.
Distribution control — Shelves and media were gated; reach was earned or bought.
Customer memory — Brand recall at the moment of choice drove preference.
What LLMs do to them
Output gets cheap — Models produce plausible drafts in seconds. Scarcity erodes.
Expertise gets summoned — Models compress domain knowledge on demand.
Comparison gets instant — Alternatives are surfaced, ranked and explained on request.
Discovery gets mediated — Agents now stand between brand and buyer.
Memory → recommendation — The customer asks the machine before they remember the brand.
Not every brand is equally exposed. Coke, Hermès, Costco remain durable. But the conditions around every business change.

From brand memory to machine recommendation.

For CMOs, the sharpest shift may not be cheaper copy or faster media planning. It is the move from brand memory to machine recommendation. For decades marketing has worked to make brands remembered by people. The new question is whether they will be recommended by machines.

The customer does not have to forget the brand for the economics to shift. The customer merely has to ask the machine first.

Fig. 03 / The Shift
How choice gets made
Yesterday
Brand Memory
Built in people. Earned through fame, awareness, mental availability and consistent expression.
LLM era
Today
Machine Recommendation
Built in systems. Earned through evidence, trusted sources, consistent behaviour and category authority.
Memory still matters — but it now competes with an intermediary. The new question is whether the brand is not only remembered, but recommended.

What Hardwired is, how its four layers work, and what the CMO does on Monday morning to make the absence of hardwiring visible inside the company.

04 What Hardwired measures

Brand in its most serious form. Not expression. Architecture.

Hardwired is the system by which a company protects its valuable difference over time. It has four connected layers.

Read bottom-up: philosophy grounds, vision directs, decisions enact, infrastructure protects. A campaign expresses the brand. These four layers govern it.
05 From story to judgment

A campaign can dramatise difference. It cannot govern it. A platform can sharpen expression. It cannot tell a company what to do when two good options collide. Hardwired treats narrative not as messaging, but as decision logic.

Philosophy defines the company's core account of itself: what it believes, where it creates value and what it refuses to become. Vision keeps that philosophy alive under changing conditions. Together they tell the company what matters, what counts as progress, and what must not be dissolved.

For CMOs, this is the shift. The future of brand is not simply better storytelling. It is better protection of the things that make the story true.

06 The paradox of efficiency

AI can improve the activity while dissolving the identity.

Fig. 05 / The Three Tests
Where LLMs apply pressure
Test 01
Production
If models make copy, code, content, analysis and design cheaper — what remains valuable?
Test 02
Decision-making
If customers ask machines before markets — why are you recommended?
Test 03
Internal operations
If AI makes the company faster — what stops it making the company more generic?
A company can produce more content, faster, with cleaner drafts and cheaper workflows, while slowly becoming less distinctive. The output improves. The edge disappears.
07 Growth without dilution

What difference can we now scale?

A hardwired brand uses AI to scale what makes it distinctive. An un-hardwired brand uses AI to make more things, faster, with less edge. More content. More variants. More automation. More activity. Less difference.

08 What CMOs should do first

Start with a difference audit.

Answering honestly is harder than it looks. Most companies discover their "difference" lives only in communications — not in the operating systems that would make it true.
Fig. 07 / The Hard Test
Where does the promise actually live?
Promise intimacy —
where is it built into service, data, CRM and decision rights?
Promise craft —
where is it protected from speed, scale and automation?
Promise value —
where does it live in pricing, sourcing, membership and customer experience?
Promise trust —

Promise innovation —
where does AI help the company extend the promise rather than simply produce noise?
The CMO's job is not to own every system. It is to ensure the company sees whether its systems protect the thing the brand asks people to believe.
09 The premium asset

As intelligence becomes cheaper, more of the economy becomes searchable, comparable, reproducible and recommendable. Difference does not become less important. It becomes harder to sustain.

Brand is not the paint on the machine. It is the part of the machine that tells the company what must not be dissolved and what can be grown without dilution.

> The Boardroom Question

When everything gets easier to copy,
what have we made difficult to dissolve —
and what can we now scale
